National rule baseline

Outbuilding Height Limits

Outbuildings such as garden rooms, sheds, garages and other detached structures can often be built under permitted development rights, but strict height limits apply. These limits are designed to ensure that outbuildings remain subordinate to the main house and do not cause visual harm to neighbouring properties.

  • Outbuildings must not exceed 4 metres in overall height if they have a dual-pitched roof.
  • Outbuildings with any other roof type must not exceed 3 metres in overall height.
  • If an outbuilding is located within 2 metres of a boundary, the maximum overall height is limited to 2.5 metres.
Why this rule matters

Height limits are a key part of permitted development rules for outbuildings in England. These restrictions ensure that detached garden structures remain modest in scale and do not dominate neighbouring gardens or block daylight. Structures such as garden offices, workshops, summerhouses and detached garages must be designed carefully to remain within these height limits. The height is measured from the highest natural ground level adjacent to the building. In practice, many homeowners choose a flat roof or low-pitched roof when building close to a boundary so they can stay within the 2.5 metre restriction.

When this usually needs a closer check: If an outbuilding exceeds permitted development height limits, full planning permission will normally be required. Additional restrictions may also apply if the property is located within a conservation area, national park, Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty, or if the house itself is a listed building.
National rule baseline

Outbuilding Position and Location Rules

Outbuildings must be located within the residential curtilage of the main house and positioned so they remain clearly ancillary to the property. The location of an outbuilding is an important factor in determining whether it qualifies as permitted development.

  • Outbuildings must be located within the residential curtilage of the dwellinghouse.
  • Outbuildings cannot be positioned forward of the principal elevation of the house.
  • The total area covered by outbuildings and other extensions must not exceed 50% of the land surrounding the original house.
Why this rule matters

Under permitted development rights, outbuildings such as garden rooms, home offices and detached garages must remain within the established residential curtilage of the property. This means the structure must be within the garden area that forms part of the house plot. Outbuildings cannot normally be constructed in front of the main front wall of the house. This rule prevents garden structures from appearing prominently in the street scene. In addition, the combined footprint of all extensions, sheds, and other outbuildings must not cover more than half of the total land around the original dwellinghouse. These rules help ensure that gardens are not overdeveloped and that outdoor space is retained.

When this usually needs a closer check: Planning permission may be required if the outbuilding is proposed in front of the house, outside the residential curtilage, or if the cumulative coverage of buildings exceeds the 50% land limit.
National rule baseline

Outbuilding Distance From Boundary Rules

The distance between an outbuilding and neighbouring property boundaries affects the permitted development height limits and may influence whether planning permission is required.

  • Outbuildings located within 2 metres of a boundary must not exceed 2.5 metres in overall height.
  • Outbuildings located more than 2 metres from a boundary may be up to 3 metres high with a flat roof or 4 metres with a dual-pitched roof.
  • Outbuildings must not cause significant overlooking or loss of privacy to neighbouring properties.
Why this rule matters

Boundary rules exist to protect neighbouring properties from overshadowing, loss of daylight and visual intrusion. When an outbuilding is built very close to a boundary fence or wall, its height must be limited to 2.5 metres regardless of roof type. This restriction is one of the most common reasons why homeowners choose a flat-roof design for garden rooms and garden offices. If the structure is located further away from the boundary, greater height allowances apply depending on the roof design. Local planning authorities may also consider whether the building would cause overlooking or appear overly dominant when viewed from neighbouring gardens.

When this usually needs a closer check: Where an outbuilding exceeds the permitted development height limits near a boundary, planning permission will normally be required. Additional restrictions may also apply in conservation areas or for listed properties.
National rule baseline

Outbuilding Roof Design Rules

The design of an outbuilding roof affects how the structure is measured for permitted development height limits and whether it complies with national planning rules.

  • Dual-pitched roofs can reach a maximum height of 4 metres under permitted development.
  • Flat, monopitch or other roof types are limited to a maximum height of 3 metres.
  • Outbuildings within 2 metres of a boundary must not exceed 2.5 metres regardless of roof design.
Why this rule matters

Roof design plays an important role in determining whether an outbuilding complies with permitted development limits. Dual-pitched roofs are allowed a slightly greater height because the ridge sits above the eaves line and can create a more traditional appearance for detached garages or larger garden buildings. Flat roofs are often used where the building is close to a boundary because they make it easier to stay within the 2.5 metre limit. The height of an outbuilding is measured from ground level to the highest point of the roof structure. Careful design of the roof profile can help homeowners maximise internal space while remaining within permitted development limits.

When this usually needs a closer check: Outbuildings that exceed the permitted development height limits for their roof design will normally require planning permission from the local planning authority.
National rule baseline

Outbuilding Materials and Appearance

The materials used for an outbuilding should complement the main house and surrounding area to ensure the structure blends appropriately within the garden setting.

  • Materials should normally be similar in appearance to those used on the existing house.
  • Outbuildings should remain visually subordinate to the main dwelling.
  • Design and materials should avoid creating a dominant or intrusive structure within the garden.
Why this rule matters

Although permitted development rules do not always strictly dictate materials for outbuildings, planning guidance generally encourages homeowners to choose finishes that complement the existing property. Timber cladding, brickwork that matches the house, or neutral render are commonly used materials for garden buildings and detached garages. Using sympathetic materials helps ensure that the outbuilding sits comfortably within the garden environment and does not appear overly prominent. Local planning authorities may take design and materials into account if an application for planning permission is required, particularly where the property is located in a sensitive area.

When this usually needs a closer check: Stricter design requirements may apply in conservation areas, national parks, or for listed buildings where preserving the character of the property and surrounding environment is a key consideration.
Local restriction signals

Important Planning Restrictions

  • Conservation areas: Outbuildings constructed to the side of houses in conservation areas may require planning permission, particularly where development would affect the character or appearance of the conservation area.
  • Listed buildings: Outbuildings within the curtilage of a listed building normally require Listed Building Consent because the structure may affect the historic significance of the property.
