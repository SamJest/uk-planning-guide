National rule baseline

Single Storey Extension Height Limits

Single storey extensions must comply with strict height restrictions under permitted development rules to ensure that the addition remains proportionate to the original dwelling and does not negatively affect neighbouring properties.

  • The maximum overall height of a single storey extension must not exceed 4 metres.
  • If the extension is within 2 metres of a boundary, the eaves height must not exceed 3 metres.
  • The extension must remain lower than the highest part of the existing roof.
  • Roof structures and features must be included when calculating the overall height.
Why this rule matters

Height limits are one of the most important design considerations when planning a single storey extension. Permitted development rules allow extensions of up to 4 metres in height, which provides sufficient headroom for typical ceiling heights while keeping the structure visually modest. Where the extension is positioned close to a neighbouring boundary, the eaves height must be reduced to a maximum of 3 metres to limit the impact on neighbouring gardens and properties. These restrictions are intended to prevent extensions from appearing overly dominant or blocking daylight to adjacent homes. Many single storey extensions use flat roofs or shallow pitched roofs to stay within these limits while still allowing for good internal space and insulation. Skylights or roof lanterns are often incorporated into the roof design to bring natural light into the extension without exceeding permitted height restrictions.

When this usually needs a closer check: Single storey extensions that exceed the permitted height limits will normally require planning permission and may be subject to additional design scrutiny.
National rule baseline

Single Storey Rear Extension Depth Limits

Permitted development rules restrict how far a single storey extension can project beyond the rear wall of the original house in order to maintain adequate garden space and protect neighbouring properties.

  • Detached houses may extend up to 4 metres beyond the original rear wall under standard permitted development rules.
  • Semi-detached and terraced houses may extend up to 3 metres beyond the original rear wall.
  • Larger extensions of up to 8 metres for detached houses and 6 metres for others may be possible through the prior approval process.
  • Depth measurements must be taken from the rear wall of the original house.
Why this rule matters

The depth of a single storey extension determines how far it extends into the rear garden. Permitted development rules allow modest extensions without the need for a full planning application, provided they remain within defined limits. Detached houses may extend up to 4 metres beyond the rear wall of the original house, while semi-detached and terraced houses are limited to 3 metres. These limits help ensure that the extension remains proportionate to the property and does not significantly reduce outdoor space. A separate larger home extension scheme allows deeper extensions if neighbouring properties are consulted and the local authority approves the proposal through a prior approval process. This allows extensions of up to 8 metres for detached houses and 6 metres for other houses.

When this usually needs a closer check: Single storey extensions that exceed these depth limits will normally require planning permission unless they qualify under the larger home extension prior approval scheme.
National rule baseline

Position of Single Storey Extensions on the Property

Single storey extensions must be positioned carefully on the property to ensure they remain behind the main front wall of the house and within the residential curtilage.

  • Single storey extensions must not extend beyond the principal elevation facing the highway.
  • The extension must remain entirely within the property boundary.
  • Extensions should be designed to minimise impact on neighbouring properties.
  • The extension should remain clearly subordinate to the original dwelling.
Why this rule matters

Planning rules require single storey extensions to be positioned behind the principal elevation of the house, which usually refers to the main front wall facing the street. This ensures that the extension does not alter the appearance of the building when viewed from the public highway. By restricting extensions to the side or rear of the property, the planning system helps maintain the character of residential streets and prevents large additions from dominating the street frontage. Positioning the extension within the rear garden also helps reduce visual impact on neighbouring properties and allows sufficient space for outdoor use. Designers often carefully place windows, doors, and walls to minimise overlooking and overshadowing issues that could affect neighbouring homes.

When this usually needs a closer check: Extensions that extend forward of the principal elevation or cross the property boundary will normally require planning permission.
National rule baseline

Roof Design for Single Storey Extensions

The roof design of a single storey extension must remain within permitted development height limits and should complement the architectural style of the existing house.

  • The roof must not exceed the maximum permitted height of the extension.
  • Flat roofs are commonly used to remain within height restrictions.
  • Pitched roofs may be used where they complement the style of the house.
  • Rooflights or lanterns may be installed provided they remain within the height limits.
Why this rule matters

Roof design is an important part of the overall appearance and functionality of a single storey extension. Many extensions use flat roofs because they help keep the structure within the permitted 4 metre height limit while allowing good internal ceiling heights. Flat roofs also provide opportunities to install rooflights or lanterns that bring natural light into the interior space. Alternatively, pitched roofs may be used to match the architectural style of the original house. Planning authorities generally expect the roof of the extension to remain visually subordinate to the main building so that the original house continues to dominate the overall appearance of the property. Careful roof design can also improve insulation, drainage, and long-term durability.

When this usually needs a closer check: Roof designs that cause the extension to exceed permitted development height limits or significantly alter the appearance of the house may require planning permission.
National rule baseline

External Materials for Single Storey Extensions

The materials used in the construction of a single storey extension must normally match or closely resemble those used on the existing house.

  • External walls should use materials similar in appearance to the original house.
  • Brickwork, render, or cladding should complement the architectural style of the property.
  • Roof materials should match or harmonise with the existing roof covering.
  • The extension should appear visually integrated with the main building.
Why this rule matters

Material selection plays an important role in ensuring that a single storey extension blends seamlessly with the existing house. Planning rules typically require the external materials used in the construction of the extension to be similar in appearance to those of the original dwelling. This usually means matching brickwork, render finishes, roof tiles, or other visible materials. Using similar materials helps the extension appear as a natural continuation of the house rather than a separate structure. In some contemporary designs contrasting materials may be used intentionally, but these proposals often require planning permission because they change the external appearance of the building. Selecting appropriate materials also improves durability and ensures the extension remains weather resistant while maintaining the architectural character of the property.

When this usually needs a closer check: In conservation areas or historic settings, planning authorities may require specific traditional materials to preserve the character of the area.
Local restriction signals

Important Planning Restrictions

  • Conservation areas: Additional planning restrictions may apply to single storey extensions in conservation areas, particularly where the extension affects the appearance of historic buildings or streets.
  • Listed buildings: Single storey extensions affecting listed buildings require listed building consent in addition to any planning permission.
