Scottish rule baseline

Panel Height and Overall Projection

Domestic solar panels are often most straightforward when they sit close to the roof or wall and avoid making the building noticeably taller or bulkier.

  • Panels should generally remain visually secondary to the building.
  • Large projecting frames or elevated freestanding arrays usually attract a closer review.
  • The more visible the installation is from the street, the more carefully its scale should be considered.
  • Associated equipment can matter if it materially changes the apparent height or bulk.
Why this rule matters

Many domestic solar schemes are treated as low-risk when the panels read as a discreet layer on the existing building. The route becomes harder when frames, mounts or associated equipment make the installation feel more dominant or visually assertive. It is usually safest to think about the combined effect of the panels and the supporting kit, not just the modules themselves.

When this usually needs a closer check: Freestanding arrays, oversized frames or highly prominent installations often need a closer planning check.
Scottish rule baseline

Roof and Wall Projection

How far solar equipment projects from the building surface can influence whether the installation stays within the simpler route.

  • Roof-mounted panels usually work best when they sit close to the existing roof plane.
  • Wall-mounted equipment should avoid looking bulky or overly prominent.
  • Installations that read as a substantial external structure rather than an attached energy measure deserve extra caution.
  • Freestanding equipment in the garden is usually more sensitive than a roof-mounted scheme.
Why this rule matters

Projection matters because it affects both appearance and amenity. A slim roof-mounted array can feel routine, while a more visibly mounted or freestanding installation can change how the property reads from public and neighbouring views. When in doubt, assess the installation as part of the building composition rather than as plant alone.

When this usually needs a closer check: Deeply projecting arrays or freestanding structures often need a closer review.
Scottish rule baseline

Siting, Garden Arrays and Neighbour Impact

Garden-based solar equipment, side elevations and boundary-facing installations often deserve a more careful amenity and visual-impact check.

  • Freestanding panels near boundaries can create stronger visual and neighbour impact concerns.
  • Installations on visible side elevations often receive more attention than roof planes facing away from the street.
  • Associated equipment, screening and access routes can all affect the wider planning picture.
  • The more the system changes the appearance of the curtilage, the more cautious the route should be.
Why this rule matters

Boundary and amenity issues are not only about overshadowing; they are also about prominence, reflectivity, screening and how the installation changes the appearance of the site. Freestanding arrays or equipment clustered near edges of the plot often need a broader site review than a simple roof-mounted system.

When this usually needs a closer check: Boundary-adjacent arrays or prominent side-facing installations often need a closer check.
Scottish rule baseline

Roof Siting and Sensitive Roof Slopes

Roof-mounted solar panels are usually judged on how well they follow the roof plane and how visible they are on sensitive elevations.

  • Panels should usually follow the roof slope rather than appear as a separate raised structure.
  • Front-facing or highly visible roof slopes tend to attract the closest review.
  • Installations on complex roof forms or near roof edges need more careful design.
  • Where the roof itself is heritage-sensitive, the route is usually less straightforward.
Why this rule matters

The easiest solar schemes generally sit flush enough to the roof that they feel integrated rather than improvised. Panels on prominent or historic roof slopes, or installations that need more conspicuous framing, can quickly move out of the simplest route. It is worth assessing visibility from the public realm early.

When this usually needs a closer check: Prominent roof slopes, heritage roofs or visually awkward panel layouts often need planning permission.
Scottish rule baseline

Appearance, Glare and Ancillary Equipment

The visual finish of the array and any associated plant can affect whether the installation remains acceptable in local views.

  • Panels should be sited and designed to minimise visual clutter where practical.
  • Inverters, cabling and other equipment should be handled as part of the design, not as an afterthought.
  • Sensitive settings often demand a more restrained appearance strategy.
  • The wider energy benefit does not remove the need to consider amenity and character.
Why this rule matters

Solar proposals often succeed most easily when the supporting equipment has been considered from the start. Even where panels themselves are acceptable, visible ancillary equipment can make the scheme feel more intrusive than necessary. A tidy design and clear siting logic usually improve the planning position.

When this usually needs a closer check: Visually cluttered installations or schemes in sensitive heritage settings often need a closer review.
Local restriction signals

Important Planning Restrictions

  • Conservation areas: Solar panels in conservation areas often require a closer design review where they affect visible roof slopes, walls or the wider character of the area.
  • Listed buildings: Solar works to listed buildings or buildings in their curtilage usually need a more careful planning and listed building consent check.
