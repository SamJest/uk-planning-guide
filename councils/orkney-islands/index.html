What often changes the answer

  • Conservation areas: In a conservation area in Scotland, normal householder permitted development rights for new hard surfaces can be curtailed where associated boundary or access works affect character, so a planning check is sensible before work starts.
  • Listed buildings: There are no ordinary householder permitted development rights for works within the curtilage of a listed building where they would affect its character, and listed building consent may also be required.

Best local starting point

Driveway in Orkney Islands

A hard surface for a driveway at a house in Scotland is usually permitted development if it is incidental to the house. Where the surface lies between the dwellinghouse and a road, the materials should be permeable or rainwater should drain to porous ground within the curtilage. A separate roads consent for a new access or footway crossing may still be needed, and these householder rights do not apply to flats.

House Extension in Orkney Islands

In Scotland the main permitted development routes are a single-storey ground floor extension, a rear ground floor extension of more than one storey and certain roof enlargements. Each route has its own limits and none should be assumed in a conservation area. On exposed coastal sites, roof profile, external finish and visual impact can carry more weight than on a routine suburban plot.

Loft Conversion in Orkney Islands

A loft conversion can be permitted development in Scotland where the roof enlargement stays below the existing roof height, is kept away from the roof edges, avoids front-facing enlargement and stays within the dormer-width and front-boundary limits. On exposed coastal sites, roof profile, external finish and visual impact can carry more weight than on a routine suburban plot.

Outbuildings in Orkney Islands

In Scotland, many sheds, detached garages and similar garden buildings can be permitted development where they remain ancillary to the house, are not used as a dwelling, stay within the garden ground limits and comply with the national siting and height rules.
