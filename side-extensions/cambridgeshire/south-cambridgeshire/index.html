National rule baseline

Side Extension Height Limits

Side extensions built under permitted development must follow specific height limits to ensure the new structure remains subordinate to the existing house and does not dominate neighbouring properties.

  • Side extensions must normally be single storey when built under permitted development.
  • The maximum overall height of the side extension must not exceed 4 metres.
  • If the extension is within 2 metres of a boundary, the eaves height must not exceed 3 metres.
  • The extension must remain lower than the highest part of the existing house.
Why this rule matters

Height limits for side extensions are designed to ensure that these additions remain visually secondary to the original dwelling. Because side extensions are often visible from the street and neighbouring properties, planning rules restrict their height and scale more tightly than many rear extensions. Under permitted development rights, side extensions must normally be single storey and must not exceed 4 metres in overall height. Where the extension is positioned close to a boundary, the eaves height must not exceed 3 metres to reduce the risk of overshadowing neighbouring gardens or properties. Designers often use flat roofs or shallow pitched roofs to remain within the permitted height limits while still providing sufficient internal ceiling height. These height controls help ensure that side extensions do not alter the character of residential streets or dominate the appearance of the existing house.

When this usually needs a closer check: Side extensions that exceed permitted height limits or include additional storeys will normally require planning permission.
National rule baseline

Rear Projection Limits for Side Extensions

Side extensions must be carefully designed so that they do not extend excessively beyond the rear wall of the original house when built under permitted development.

  • Side extensions must not extend beyond the rear wall of the original house by more than 3 metres.
  • The projection should remain modest compared with the depth of the house.
  • The extension must remain proportionate to the original building.
  • The extension should not significantly reduce usable garden space.
Why this rule matters

Although side extensions primarily add space along the side of a property, they may also extend slightly beyond the rear wall of the original house. Planning rules limit this rear projection to prevent side extensions from effectively becoming large rear extensions that extend deep into the garden. The maximum permitted rear projection is typically 3 metres beyond the rear wall of the original house. This restriction helps ensure that the extension remains secondary to the main building and does not result in excessive development of the plot. Keeping the extension within these limits also helps preserve adequate garden space and reduces the potential impact on neighbouring properties, particularly in areas where houses are closely spaced.

When this usually needs a closer check: If a side extension projects further than 3 metres beyond the rear wall of the original house, planning permission will normally be required.
National rule baseline

Width Restrictions for Side Extensions

Planning rules limit the width of side extensions to ensure they remain subordinate additions rather than creating the appearance of a separate dwelling.

  • The width of the side extension must not exceed half the width of the original house.
  • The extension should remain visually subordinate to the main building.
  • The original side elevation of the house should remain clearly identifiable.
  • The extension must not extend beyond the principal elevation facing the highway.
Why this rule matters

Side extensions are subject to strict width restrictions because they are often visible from public streets and neighbouring properties. Under permitted development rules, the width of the extension must not exceed half the width of the original house. This ensures that the extension remains clearly secondary to the existing building and does not create the appearance of a second dwelling or a significantly enlarged property. The extension must also be located behind the principal elevation of the house, which usually refers to the front wall facing the street. Keeping the extension behind this line helps preserve the visual character of residential streets and prevents large structures from appearing prominently at the front of properties.

When this usually needs a closer check: Side extensions that exceed half the width of the original house or extend forward of the principal elevation will normally require planning permission.
National rule baseline

Roof Design for Side Extensions

The roof design of a side extension must remain proportionate to the original house and must comply with permitted development height restrictions.

  • The roof must not exceed the highest part of the existing house.
  • Roof pitches should normally match or complement the existing roof.
  • Flat roofs are commonly used to keep the extension within permitted height limits.
  • Roof features should remain visually subordinate to the main roof.
Why this rule matters

Roof design plays an important role in how well a side extension integrates with the existing property. Planning authorities generally expect the roof of the extension to complement the architectural style of the main house. Many side extensions use flat roofs because they help keep the structure comfortably within the permitted height limit of 4 metres while still providing adequate internal ceiling height. Alternatively, pitched roofs may be used where they reflect the style of the existing building. In all cases, the roof of the extension must remain lower than the highest part of the house so that the original building remains the dominant architectural feature. Careful roof design can also improve the appearance of the extension when viewed from neighbouring properties or public spaces.

When this usually needs a closer check: Roof designs that exceed permitted height limits or significantly alter the appearance of the house may require planning permission.
National rule baseline

External Materials for Side Extensions

Side extensions must normally use materials that match or closely resemble the materials used on the existing house.

  • External walls should use materials similar in appearance to the existing house.
  • Brickwork, render, or cladding should match the style of the original building.
  • Roof materials should complement the existing roof covering.
  • The extension should visually integrate with the main dwelling.
Why this rule matters

Material selection is an important planning consideration for side extensions because these structures are often visible from the street. Permitted development rules typically require that materials used in the exterior construction of the extension are similar in appearance to those used on the existing house. This usually means matching brickwork, render finishes, roof tiles, or other visible materials. Using similar materials helps the extension blend seamlessly with the original building and maintain the character of the surrounding neighbourhood. In some modern designs contrasting materials may be used intentionally, but these proposals are more likely to require planning permission because they change the external appearance of the property. Careful material selection also helps ensure the extension remains durable and weather resistant while preserving the architectural integrity of the house.

When this usually needs a closer check: In conservation areas or historic neighbourhoods, planning authorities may require specific traditional materials to preserve the character of the area.
Local restriction signals

Important Planning Restrictions

  • Conservation areas: Side extensions in conservation areas may require planning permission, particularly if they are visible from public viewpoints or affect the character of the area.
  • Listed buildings: Extensions to listed buildings require listed building consent in addition to any planning permission.
