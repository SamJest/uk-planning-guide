Temporary Buildings Planning Permission

With temporary buildings, the label often sounds more helpful than it really is. What matters is whether the use, duration and physical setup still attract ordinary planning scrutiny.

Working position

A building is not automatically easier just because it is described as temporary. The planning answer usually turns on purpose, duration, siting, services and how permanent the structure looks in practice.

What changes it fastest

Long duration, fixed foundations, independent use, repeated occupation and sensitive sites are the reasons temporary structures often need a closer planning check.

Safest next step

How This Is Usually Read In Practice

This section is written as a working professional read: what tends to matter first, where assumptions break down and when the safer move is to verify before spending more.

Last reviewed 11 April 2026 Official sources checked Expert guide, not legal advice

Professional reading

The word 'temporary' does not settle much on its own. Planners usually look at purpose, duration, services and how removable the structure really is in practice.

Worked example

A short-lived event structure with a credible removal date and minimal servicing can sometimes stay easier to justify. A building that is fixed down, serviced like a normal room and kept on site for an open-ended period usually will not.

Where people get caught out

People often assume that demountable construction or a short lease automatically makes the planning route easy, even when the building behaves like a permanent one.

Stop and verify when the structure has foundations, utilities, regular occupation or a removal plan that would be hard to defend if challenged.

The Broad Planning Position, The Main Tripwires And The Evidence Worth Gathering

What to gather before you spend more

  • A clear statement of how long the structure will stay on site and what event, project or operational need justifies it.
  • The physical specification: foundations, services, anchoring and whether the building can realistically be removed cleanly.
  • The use pattern, including whether people will sleep, work, store equipment or treat it as a normal building for an extended period.
